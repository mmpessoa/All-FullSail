Week 2 Discussion: Accessibility

 

What are some of the ways that you could change your HTML on your Demo app to be more accessible? (Remember, you can use the Section tag with jQuery, for instance)

There are four disability areas that can be addressed with HTML5 features. For the hearing impaired, I can provide text alternatives to alert sound or subtitles for video content.

Mobility problems are harder to address. I would have to make sure that clickable links can be triggered by the keyboard or mouse, and not by touch alone. Also, there shouldn’t be any time limits to input information, and content cannot auto-refresh. Users with mobility problems need more time to complete tasks. There’s also the possibility of using voice recognition to advance through menus, but that’s way beyond my current skillset.

Cognitive difficulties can be addressed as well. If I want a webpage/app to make sense to a six-year-old, a semi-literate adult, or someone that speaks English as a second language, I would focus on clear, legible icons and simple sans-serif fonts. I’d avoid technical jargon, slang or idioms that might confuse a non-mainstream user.

The solution for visual problems combines some of the solutions above. Legibility will help both the cognitively disabled and the visually impaired. Contrast between text and background can be fixed without special tools, and there are online tools that can scan a site and flag areas where the colors would cause issues for those with different types of colorblindness.

Visit a video service on the web (like YouTube) and search for "Screen Reader Demo" and "Mobile Screen Reader" - what are some of the common frustrations (or common support mechanisms) that you see among the examples that are given - how could you fix them (or use them)?

The main problems that I saw in the demos I viewed (https://vimeo.com/8369435 ,  http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DZtCjS1Yz1g  and http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=bZKLPMsb2g0), were lack of control over the speed of the spoken text, different voices and accents being used in the same sentence and mispronunciations of words, inability to control the size of text or color contrast in apps other than default system apps. The only problem that I think I could fix is to make sure that any images I use are accurately annotated with ALT tags that screen readers can speak. Links can also be given names and IDs that directly relate to their function – i.e. “Submit Form” instead of “Click Me,” or something like that.

Devices: Please list two devices (and some of their features) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

The Tobii PCEye is an eye-tracking controller that allows people who cannot use their hands to control their computer via eye movements.

http://www.disabled-world.com/assistivedevices/computer/tobii-pceye.php

There is a lip-reading program created at a Malaysian university that can interpret emotions. This is both amazing and scary. I would think that it would be inaccurate if used cross-culturally since there are tremendous differences in how emotions are visually expressed in Eastern and Western cultures.

http://www.disabled-world.com/assistivedevices/computer/lipreading.php

Mobile Apps: Please list two apps (either Android or iOS, or both) that are made specifically to support Accessibility.

Verizon Wireless has an Android app that has a variety of aids for the visually impaired using voice synthesis and voice recognition:

  • Make and place calls
  • Listen to text messages
  • Control speech pitch and rate.

http://www.disabled-world.com/assistivedevices/apps/verizon-app.php

An iOS an app called TextFaster allows users with mobility restrictions to text faster and more accurately using large keys and touch gestures.

http://videos.disabled-world.com/video/392/text-faster-iphone-app
